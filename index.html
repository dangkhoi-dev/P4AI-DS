4. Numerical Feature Distributions
Fig 4.1 – Age & Fare Distribution. Age approximates a normal distribution centered around 30. Fare is extremely right-skewed (most paid under £50, but some paid over £500), indicating heavy-tailed wealth disparity on board.

Key Insights: Continuous Features

  • Age (mean ≈ 29.7, std ≈ 14.5): Peaks around 20–35 years — the majority of passengers were young adults. A secondary peak near 0–5 represents children.
  • Fare (mean ≈ £32, max ≈ £512): Median fare is only ~£14 but extreme outliers exceeding £500 indicate first-class luxury cabins. Log transformation is essential before modeling.
  • Skewness: Fare skewness > 4.0 — a textbook example requiring normalization or binning for tree-based models and definitely log-scaling for linear models.
Fig 4.2 – Family Size Distributions (SibSp & Parch). The vast majority of passengers traveled alone or with minimal family — zero siblings/spouses is the dominant class for SibSp; zero parents/children for Parch.

Key Insights: Family Indicators

  • SibSp (max=8, mean=0.52): ~68% of passengers had SibSp=0 (traveled alone or without siblings). The tail extends to 8, representing large families who may have clustered in lower classes.
  • Parch (max=6, mean=0.38): ~76% had Parch=0. This combined with SibSp allows FamilySize engineering: FamilySize = SibSp + Parch + 1, which often shows that mid-size families (2–4) survived at higher rates.
5. Categorical Feature Distributions
Fig 5.1 – Categorical Breakdown. Passenger class, gender, embarkation port, and cabin availability split. Third class dominated (~55%), males outnumbered females ~2:1, and Southampton was the primary embarkation point (~72%).
Fig 5.2 – Ticket Prefix Frequency. Extracting alphanumeric prefixes from ticket numbers reveals systematic groupings: "PC" prefixes often belong to first-class passengers, while numeric-only tickets are more common in 3rd class.

Key Insights: Categorical Features

  • Pclass: 491 passengers in 3rd class vs 216 in 1st. This socio-economic proxy is one of the strongest predictors of survival.
  • Sex: 577 male, 314 female. The "women and children first" maritime protocol creates a powerful gender–survival signal.
  • Embarked: 72% boarded at Southampton (S), 19% at Cherbourg (C), 9% at Queenstown (Q). Cherbourg passengers had higher average fares, correlating with higher survival.
  • Ticket Prefix: Can be engineered into a high-cardinality categorical feature useful for gradient boosting models.
6. Correlation Analysis
Fig 6.1 – Pearson Correlation Heatmap. Numerical features correlation matrix. Pclass and Fare show strong negative correlation (r ≈ −0.55) — higher class means lower fare index. Survived correlates moderately with Fare (r ≈ +0.26) and negatively with Pclass (r ≈ −0.34).

Key Insights: Feature Correlations

  • Pclass ↔ Fare (r = −0.55): Strong negative correlation — 1st class passengers paid significantly more. These two features carry redundant but complementary information.
  • Survived ↔ Fare (r = +0.26): Higher fare → higher survival. As a proxy for wealth and class, this makes intuitive sense.
  • Survived ↔ Pclass (r = −0.34): Lower Pclass value (1 = best) → higher survival probability. One of the clearest signals in the dataset.
  • Age ↔ Pclass (r = −0.37): Older passengers tended to be in higher classes — indicating age and wealth were correlated in this era.
7. Survival Rate by Feature
Fig 7.1 – Survival Rates by Gender & Class. Female survival rate ~74%, male ~19%. 1st class survival ~63%, 2nd class ~47%, 3rd class ~24%. Gender is the single strongest individual predictor.

Key Insights: Survival Patterns

  • Gender effect: Females survived at 3.9× the rate of males — the single most discriminative feature in the dataset. Any classifier should learn this boundary first.
  • Class gradient: Survival rate drops from 63% (1st) → 47% (2nd) → 24% (3rd) — reflecting both lifeboat access proximity and crew prioritization.
  • Interaction: 1st class female survival rate was ~97% vs 3rd class male at just ~13% — suggesting interaction features (Sex × Pclass) will dramatically boost model performance.
8. Outlier Detection
Fig 8.1 – Box Plots for Outlier Detection. Fare shows extreme positive outliers (IQR method identifies values > £65 as outliers). Age has mild outliers in the 60–80 range. SibSp and Parch show several outlier family-size records.

Key Insights: Outlier Impact

  • Fare outliers (max = £512.33): These belong to luxury suite passengers. While genuine data, they will inflate feature means and distort linear models — log-transform or winsorization is recommended.
  • SibSp = 8 records: A family of 11 aboard — all in 3rd class, none survived. This represents a genuine but extreme record that should be preserved with the "IsAlone" feature engineering.

🔍 Tabular EDA — Summary of Findings

  • Demographic Disparity: Gender and Pclass are the two most powerful predictors. The "women and children first" protocol is clearly reflected in the survival rates, making Sex the #1 feature.
  • Socio-Economic Signal: Fare and Pclass are strongly anti-correlated, both encoding wealth. Together they predict survival more powerfully than either alone — expect interaction terms to boost model performance.
  • Missing Data Strategy: Cabin (77% missing) should be binarized; Age (20% missing) should be imputed using grouped medians by Sex and Pclass; Embarked (2 missing) can use mode-fill.
  • Feature Engineering Opportunities: Title extraction from Name, FamilySize = SibSp + Parch + 1, IsAlone flag, Cabin deck letter, and ticket prefix can all meaningfully improve predictive models.
  • Distribution Shapes: Fare's extreme right-skew demands log-transformation before any distance or gradient-based algorithm is applied.

5. Class Distribution & Imbalance
Fig 5.1 – Class Frequency & Proportion. 4,825 Ham (86.6%) vs 747 Spam (13.4%). A ~6.5:1 imbalance ratio that will cause naive models to predict "Ham" for everything and still achieve 86% accuracy — requiring careful evaluation with precision/recall/F1.

Key Insights: Class Imbalance

  • The severe imbalance (~87% Ham) means accuracy is a misleading metric — a model predicting "Ham" for every message achieves 86.6% accuracy but catches 0% of spam.
  • Recommended strategies: SMOTE oversampling of the Spam class, class-weight adjustment in sklearn (class_weight='balanced'), or threshold tuning on predicted probabilities.
  • Evaluation should prioritize Recall for Spam class — false negatives (missed spam) are more costly than false positives (ham flagged as spam).

  • The two-feature combination (word_count, char_count) creates a low-dimensional, interpretable feature pair that can power lightweight production classifiers.
9. Vocabulary Analysis — Top Words

10. TF-IDF Unigram Analysis
Fig 10.1 – Top TF-IDF Weighted Unigrams by Class. Spam: "free", "txt", "mobile", "claim", "prize". Ham: "ok", "good", "come", "love", "lt". The vocabulary separation is dramatic — Spam uses commercial/promotional language; Ham uses personal/relational language.

Key Insights: TF-IDF Discriminative Words

  • Spam signature words: "free" (appears in 44% of Spam, only 0.3% of Ham), "txt", "call", "win", "prize", "claim" — strongly promotional and action-oriented.
  • Ham signature words: "ok", "will", "come", "love", "good", "know" — conversational, relational, and emotionally neutral/positive.
  • TF-IDF successfully surfaces these discriminative tokens by penalizing words common to both classes — a Naive Bayes classifier trained purely on TF-IDF unigrams achieves ~97% F1.
11. TF-IDF Bigram Analysis
Fig 11.1 – Top TF-IDF Bigram Features by Class. Spam bigrams: "free entry", "customer service", "reply stop", "call now". Ham bigrams: "good morning", "let know", "hope you", "thank you". Bigrams capture complete promotional phrases that unigrams would split.

Key Insights: Bigram Context

  • Bigrams capture complete promotional phrases: "free entry", "win prize", "claim reward" — patterns that a unigram model treats as two separate weak signals, but bigrams recognize as one strong signal.
  • "Reply STOP" is a legally mandated opt-out phrase in SMS marketing — its presence alone is a near-perfect spam indicator with near-zero false positive rate.
  • Adding bigrams to a unigram TF-IDF model typically improves F1 by 1–2 points and reduces false negatives for creative spam that avoids obvious unigrams.
12. Word Cloud Visualization
Fig 12.1 – Word Clouds: Spam (left) vs. Ham (right). The visual contrast is immediate: Spam is dominated by capitalized urgency words (FREE, CALL, WIN, PRIZE, MOBILE); Ham reflects natural human conversation (ok, got, will, come, time, good).

Key Insights: Semantic Distance

  • Cosine Similarity between Spam and Ham TF-IDF centroids ≈ 0.0075 — essentially orthogonal in high-dimensional space. The vocabularies are fundamentally distinct.
  • This near-zero cosine similarity mathematically justifies using linear classifiers (Logistic Regression, LinearSVM) as strong first baselines — the classes are approximately linearly separable in TF-IDF space.
  • Word clouds visually confirm that all-caps words ("FREE", "WIN", "URGENT") are a Spam hallmark — a simple regex feature (count of ALL_CAPS words) can serve as a powerful engineered feature.

  • Cả hai features nên được đưa vào feature vector cùng TF-IDF thay vì chỉ chọn một.
14. Uppercase Ratio & Special Character Analysis
Fig 14.1 – Feature Means by Class: Uppercase ratio, !, $, URL, phone number, digit presence. Spam có tỉ lệ uppercase, dấu chấm than và chứa số điện thoại cao hơn Ham rõ rệt — đây là các engineered features dễ tính toán nhưng hiệu quả cao.

Key Insights: Ký tự đặc biệt là dấu hiệu spam

  • Uppercase ratio: Spam trung bình ~15–20% ký tự viết hoa (FREE, WIN, URGENT) so với Ham ~3–5% — tỉ lệ này một mình đạt accuracy ~80%.
  • Exclamation marks (!) và dollar signs ($): xuất hiện gấp nhiều lần trong spam — phản ánh ngôn ngữ khẩn cấp và thương mại.
  • Phone numbers (chuỗi số ≥ 5 chữ số): gần như chỉ có trong spam (premium-rate numbers để thu phí người dùng).
  • Kết hợp các regex features này với TF-IDF tạo ra feature vector mạnh mà không cần deep learning.
15. Vocabulary Richness (Type-Token Ratio)
Fig 15.1 – Unique word count, TTR, và average word length theo class. Ham có vocabulary phong phú hơn Spam (TTR cao hơn) do đề cập nhiều chủ đề cá nhân khác nhau. Spam lặp lại từ vựng cố định (free, win, call, txt) nên TTR thấp hơn.

Key Insights: Vocabulary Diversity

  • Type-Token Ratio (TTR) của Ham cao hơn Spam: Ham đề cập nhiều chủ đề cá nhân đa dạng, trong khi Spam xoay quanh một tập từ vựng cố định (quảng cáo).
  • Average word length: Spam có xu hướng dùng từ ngắn hơn (txt, ur, free, win) — thông điệp được tối ưu hóa để đọc nhanh trên màn hình nhỏ.
  • TTR thấp của Spam là bằng chứng định lượng cho thấy spam messages được sinh ra từ template cố định với một số slot thay thế — điều này giải thích tại sao rule-based filters hoạt động tốt.

💬 Text EDA — Summary of Findings

  • Class Imbalance Critical: 86.6% Ham vs 13.4% Spam demands imbalance-aware strategies. Use SMOTE or class_weight='balanced'; evaluate with F1-score for Spam, not overall accuracy.
  • Length is a Free Feature: Message word count and character count alone achieve solid classification baselines. These should always be included as explicit features alongside TF-IDF.
  • TF-IDF is Highly Effective: The near-orthogonal vocabulary distributions (cosine sim ≈ 0.0075) mean even simple linear models with TF-IDF features will achieve excellent results (>97% F1).
  • Bigrams Add Context: Phrase-level patterns ("free entry", "reply stop") capture advertising language patterns that single tokens cannot. Always include at least bigrams in the n-gram range.
  • Preprocessing Pipeline: Lowercase → remove punctuation → tokenize → remove stopwords → TF-IDF (1,2)-gram → dimensionality reduction (optional SVD). This standard pipeline is well-suited to this dataset.

  • No resampling (SMOTE), class-weight tuning, or threshold adjustment is required at the dataset level — the training objective can use standard cross-entropy loss directly.
  • The balanced split ensures that any accuracy improvement directly reflects better feature learning rather than exploitation of class frequency.
6. RGB Channel Distribution
Fig 6.1 – Pixel Intensity Distribution per RGB Channel. All three channels follow broad, roughly bell-shaped distributions centered around 110–130 (out of 255). Red channel skews slightly higher than Blue/Green — typical of natural outdoor scenes with warm ambient lighting.

  • Mean pixel values: R ≈ 125, G ≈ 123, B ≈ 114. The slight red dominance reflects warm natural lighting in outdoor photography scenarios.
  • Preprocessing implication: Per-channel normalization — subtract channel mean, divide by channel std — is strongly recommended before feeding into any neural network. This centers distributions and accelerates gradient descent convergence.
  • Channel correlation: R, G, B channels are highly correlated for natural images. PCA whitening can decorrelate channels and further improve convergence, though modern BatchNorm layers mitigate this need.
  • Color as a class signal: Frog images are green-heavy; ship images are blue-heavy; horse images lean toward brown/warm tones. Color histograms can serve as a simple, interpretable baseline feature before deep learning.

🖼️ Image EDA — Summary of Findings

  • Balanced & Clean: CIFAR-10 requires minimal preprocessing — no missing values, no class imbalance, standardized 32×32 resolution. It is an ideal clean benchmark dataset for algorithm comparison.
  • Low Resolution Challenge: The 32×32 constraint forces models to rely on shape, color, and texture statistics rather than fine detail. Architectures like ResNet-20 and VGG-10 were benchmarked specifically against this constraint.
  • Color as Signal: Distinct per-class RGB profiles (blue-dominant ships, green-dominant frogs) mean simple color histogram features achieve ~40–50% accuracy — a useful non-trivial baseline before deploying CNNs.
  • Preprocessing Pipeline: uint8 → float32 division by 255 → per-channel mean subtraction → per-channel std normalization → optional random horizontal flip + crop augmentation for training robustness.
  • Modeling Direction: CNNs (especially ResNet/VGG/EfficientNet variants) are the go-to architectures. State-of-the-art on CIFAR-10 exceeds 99% accuracy with ViT and WideResNet models, providing strong upper-bound context.

What is Multimodal Data?

  • Multimodal datasets pair images with natural language captions — requiring models to learn cross-modal alignment between visual features and linguistic representations.
  • These datasets enable tasks like Image Captioning (generate text from image), Visual Question Answering (VQA) (answer questions about image content), and Image-Text Retrieval (find matching image for a query).
  • EDA on both modalities independently — and their joint relationship — is critical for understanding annotation quality, coverage gaps, and preprocessing requirements before training.

3. Image Dimension Analysis
Fig 3.1 – Distribution of Image Width & Height. Unlike CIFAR-10's fixed 32×32, real-world images have highly variable dimensions. Landscape orientation (width > height) dominates. The broad spread indicates images from diverse cameras and sources.

Key Insights: Image Geometry

  • Variable resolution: Images range from small thumbnails (100×80) to high-resolution photos (1200×900+). A fixed resize strategy is mandatory for batched neural network training.
  • Landscape dominance: Width > Height for the majority of images — this means naive square cropping (center crop) would discard more of the image content than aspect-ratio-preserving resize with padding.
  • Recommended preprocessing: Resize to 224×224 with padding to preserve aspect ratio, or resize-then-center-crop. Pre-trained ViT/CLIP models expect specific resolutions (224×224 or 336×336) — match their expected input.
  • Outlier handling: Extremely small images (< 50px on any axis) should be flagged — they contain insufficient visual information for feature extraction and may degrade batch normalization stability.
4. RGB Channel Distribution
Fig 4.1 – Pixel Intensity Distribution per RGB Channel (Multimodal Image Corpus). Broader and more variable than CIFAR-10 — reflecting the diversity of real-world photography including indoor, outdoor, close-up, and landscape scenes.

Key Insights: Visual Statistics

  • The broader pixel distribution (compared to CIFAR-10) reflects higher photographic diversity — indoor scenes (dark), outdoor scenes (bright), and mixed lighting all contribute to a flatter intensity histogram.
  • Bimodal intensity patterns in some channels suggest the dataset contains both high-key (bright/overexposed) and low-key (dark/underexposed) images — brightness normalization is important.
  • Pre-trained model normalization: Models like CLIP and BLIP were trained with specific ImageNet normalization (mean=[0.485, 0.456, 0.406], std=[0.229, 0.224, 0.225]) — use these exact values for transfer learning rather than computing dataset-specific statistics.
5. Caption Length — Box Plot Analysis
Fig 5.1 – Box Plot of Caption Word Count. Compact IQR (8–13 words) with several high-length outliers (30+ words). Median ≈ 10 words. The tight central tendency indicates consistent annotation guidelines despite the outlier long captions.

Key Insights: Caption Length Box Plot

  • IQR ≈ 8–13 words: 50% of all captions fall between 8 and 13 words — concise, focused descriptions of image content.
  • Outlier captions (30+ words): These verbose captions likely describe complex multi-subject scenes. They should be truncated to max 25 tokens during tokenization to avoid excessive padding and memory waste.
  • The compact IQR with sparse outliers suggests annotations followed a consistent length guideline — good sign for annotation quality and label noise levels.
6. Caption Length — Histogram Distribution
Fig 6.1 – Histogram of Caption Word Counts. Right-skewed distribution peaking at 8–12 words. The long tail extends to 35+ words. This informs the choice of maximum sequence length for Transformer-based models.

Key Insights: Tokenization Planning

  • The peak at 8–12 words means a max_length = 20 tokens captures approximately 90% of all captions without truncation — optimal for training efficiency.
  • A max_length = 25 tokens covers ~97% of captions — the recommended setting for production models to minimize information loss.
  • BPE/WordPiece tokenizers (used by BERT, GPT-2) will slightly inflate word counts due to subword splitting — add 20–30% buffer to word-count-based estimates when setting token max_length.
7. Caption Vocabulary — Top Words
Fig 7.1 – Most Frequent Words in Captions (After Stopword Removal). "man", "woman", "group", "standing", "holding", "street", "dog", "white", "playing" dominate — revealing a human-centric, action-focused dataset with outdoor settings.

Key Insights: Caption Vocabulary

  • Human-centric vocabulary: "man" and "woman" are the top two content words — the dataset is dominated by photographs of people, as expected from real-world photography collections (e.g., Flickr, MS-COCO).
  • Spatial & relational language: Prepositions ("in front of", "next to", "on top of") and action verbs ("walking", "holding", "sitting") dominate the middle-frequency range — indicating captions describe spatial relationships and actions, not just object labels.
  • A vocabulary of approximately 10,000–15,000 unique tokens is expected after lowercasing and minimal cleaning — well-suited for word embedding approaches (Word2Vec, GloVe) without requiring subword tokenization.

🔗 Multimodal EDA — Summary of Findings

  • Cross-Modal Quality: Both image and text modalities exhibit high internal consistency — variable-resolution clean images paired with well-formed English captions that follow consistent length guidelines. Annotation quality appears high.
  • Image Preprocessing: Aspect-ratio-preserving resize to 224×224 + per-channel normalization using ImageNet statistics. Filter out images smaller than 50px to avoid low-information samples destabilizing batch normalization.
  • Caption Preprocessing: Lowercase → punctuation removal → tokenization → max_length=25 truncation → padding. Remove or flag captions with anomalous char/word ratios.
  • Human-Centric Dataset: The vocabulary is dominated by people-related nouns and action verbs — indicating this is likely derived from a general-purpose photography dataset (Flickr-30K style or MS-COCO). Models benefit strongly from person detection priors.
  • Modeling Direction: Pre-trained vision-language models (CLIP, BLIP, LLaVA) will dramatically outperform training from scratch given this dataset's alignment with ImageNet/Common Crawl pre-training distributions. Fine-tuning a pre-trained model is the recommended approach.
